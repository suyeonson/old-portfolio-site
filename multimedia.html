Database Research


During my one-year fellowship with the Medill Justice Project, I researched, compiled and verified the components of over 1,000 court cases involving a charge regarding Shaken-Baby Syndrome, a condition the validity of which divides the medical community.

Characterized by a trifecta of symptoms that may indicate the child was a victim of violent shaking, SBS has been used as a legal tool of criminalization that some experts are now questioning. My research included speaking with national experts, tapping into existing databases in order to identify and outline the nuances of this largely unexplored issue of criminal justice.

The result was the launch of a database on which MJP fellows and graduate students at the Robert R. McCormick School of Engineering and Applied Science collaborated to launch.

In the summer of 2013, MJP published an article that concluded males have been and are more likely to be accused of having violently shaken a baby. My contribution to the database was used as a basis for the report, and is credited at the bottom of the article.
